Research Projects & Interests

Remotely sensing lake water quality


Satellite data are able to characterize water quality parameters at much broader spatial scales and higher temporal resolutions than is achievable with in situ sampling alone. My work pairs in situ lake measurements with satellite imagery to develop predictive models of water quality in lakes. I am interested in tracking how changes in metrics such as chlorophyll-a vary in a lake at different time scales, from within a season to across decades. I am also interested tracking spatial patterns in chl-a across a lake surface - e.g., do blooms occur predictably in certain areas of the lake, and is that changing through time? I am also working on a project pairing satellite imagery with measurements of cyanobacteria-specific pigments within different cell size fractions to improve our predictions of cyanobacterial abundance. Ultimately, we are aiming to develop more robust models that enable us to potentially predict not only cyanobacteria abundance, but cyanotoxin conditions as well. Cyanotoxins in food webs


Cyanobacteria and their toxins are of increasing concern worldwide for their detrimental effects on human health, as well as the environmental health of aquatic and terrestrial ecosystems. A major focus of my work has been investigating the transfer and accumulation of cyanotoxins in aquatic food webs in arctic lakes of Greenland. I have found that cyanotoxins in these systems are transferred within multiple components of the aquatic benthic and pelagic food webs. I am also especially interested in exploring how cyanotoxins are transferred from aquatic to terrestrial systems (and potentially the reverse), through mechanisms such as emerging insects, transfer through plants, and aerosolization.



Benthic cyanobacteria


In many polar lakes and ponds, benthic cyanobacteria represent an important component of the ecosystem. While many of these systems lack conspicuous surface blooms, cyanobacteria are often abundant and productive as benthic mats and multicellular colonies residing on the bottom of the lake. I am interested in the role benthic communites play in these ecosystems, particularlly in the extent to which they contribute cyanotoxins to the aquatic food web. My work studying cyanobacteria of the genus Nostoc (aka "sea tomatoes") in Greenlandic lakes has shown that benthic colonies contain cyanotoxins and may be important in releasing these toxins into aquatic ecosystems. Benthic cyanobacteria are not limited to polar environments and I am also interested in how toxin production varies in benthic communities in temperate lakes.


Water to air: cyano-aerosolization


Aerosolization of cyanobacteria and their toxins represents a potentially important route of toxin exposure and a potential mechanism of dispersal of cyanobacterial cells across landscapes, yet the environmental factors driving bioaerosol production in freshwater systems are not yet well described. I am working on developing techniques for capturing and quantifying aerosolized cells from aquatic and terrestrial substrates in temperate and polar regions to improve our understanding of how cyanobacterial cells and toxins may be transported by this process. I also use controlled laboratory and mesocosm experiments to tease apart the potential environmental (e.g., temperature, humidiity) and biological (e.g., composition of the food web) mechanisms influencing aerosolization rates.
