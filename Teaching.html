Teaching & Outreach



Teaching & Mentorship

Teaching is one of the most fun and rewarding aspects of being a scientist. I have taught seven different courses between Dartmouth College and Colby-Sawyer College, ranging from introductory classes such as Introduction to Environmental Science and Anatomy and Physiology, to intermediate and advanced courses like Field Methods in Ecology, Physiological Ecology, and Aquatic Ecology. I love bringing students into the field to observe natural processes firsthand, and to experience a wide range of research methods and approaches in diverse habitats.

I have had the plasure of teaching high school students from Greenland, Denmark, and the US through fellowships with the NSF IGERT (Integrative Graduate Education and Research Traineeship) and JSEP (Joint Science Education Program) programs in Kangerlussuaq, Greenland. I have also mentored eight undergraduate student research projects and I am always looking for enthusiastic students to assist me with research in the lab and in the field!



Community Outreach

I strongly believe that scientific progress depends on diverse perspectives and contributions from the entire community. I enjoy communicating scientific ideas to non-scientific audiences and helping to engage community scientists in hands-on research and discussions. I take part in training community scientists and water quality managers in lake monitoring techniques through the Cyanobacterial Monitoring Collaborative (CMC) and through local lake associations.

I am currently collaborating with the Lake Sunapee Protective Association (LSPA) to develop a community scientist cyanobacteria monitoring program. The program trains community members how to collect, count, and identify important types of cyanobacteria in the lake using customized sampling kits and counting protocols (check out the CyanoCounter). The resulting data are helping us track changes in the abundance and composition of cyanobacteria in Lake Sunapee. Additionally, the community scientists sample on days with known satellite overpasses to enable pairing of the in-situ data with satellite imagery, which I'm using to improve algorithms for remotely sensing cyanobacterial abundance in the lake (see Research Interests).
